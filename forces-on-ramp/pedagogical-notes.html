📐 Forces on a Ramp

Student-Centered Design Notes & Lesson Context

🚀 Launch the Applet

🎯 Where This Came From

Original Lesson Context: This applet was designed to support McKenzie Sorce's Hot Wheels F=ma lesson, which she taught during her field placement. In her original lesson, students used a PhET simulation to explore forces on inclined planes, but McKenzie identified several problems:

  • The PhET sim was too long — students didn't finish during class time
  • Students procrastinated on completing the simulation questions
  • Even engaged students gave one-word answers instead of explanations
  • The connection between the simulation and real-world F=ma was unclear

McKenzie wanted a tool that was more focused, more visual, and more directly connected to the physics concepts her students were learning.

What We Kept from PhET

  • ✅ Interactive force vector visualization
  • ✅ Adjustable parameters (angle, mass, friction)
  • ✅ Real-time physics calculations

What We Cut

  • ❌ Overly detailed controls that distract from core concepts
  • ❌ Lengthy question prompts embedded in the simulation
  • ❌ Features not directly related to F=ma (like thermal energy displays)

🛠️ Design Decisions & Rationale

1. PhET-Style Visual Design

Why: McKenzie's students were already familiar with PhET simulations. Using similar visual language (realistic textures, sky-blue background, wooden crate) reduces cognitive load and helps students transfer their existing mental models.

Alternative considered: Abstract geometric design with dark theme — rejected because it felt too "math-tool" and less relatable to physical Hot Wheels experiments.

2. Draggable Crate + "Release!" Button

Why: Students learn by doing, not just observing. Dragging the crate to set potential energy makes energy conservation tangible — they see PE → KE conversion happen in real-time on the energy bar.

Pedagogical goal: Students should predict what will happen before pressing Release, then compare their prediction to the simulation. This builds the inquiry loop: Predict → Test → Explain.

3. Dynamic Force Vector Scaling

Why: At high masses (100 kg) or steep angles (45°), force vectors would extend off-screen if drawn at a fixed scale. The applet automatically scales vectors to fit the canvas while maintaining visual proportions.

Trade-off: Students might not realize the vectors are scaled. Consider adding a note in class discussion about proportional vs. absolute magnitude.

4. Friction Feedback Message

Why: When friction is too strong (high μk, low angle), the crate won't move — but students might think the simulation is broken. The yellow message box ("💪 Friction is too strong! The crate won't budge.") turns a potential point of confusion into a teaching moment about static vs. kinetic friction.

5. Side Control Panel (Instead of Below)

Why: Keeps controls visible while manipulating the simulation. Students don't have to scroll to adjust parameters, which maintains focus on the physics rather than the interface.

⚠️ Known Limitations & Areas for Improvement

Issues We Know About

  1. Angle labeling is unclear — The degree marker (e.g., "25°") doesn't clearly indicate what's being measured. Could benefit from a more intuitive visual cue (maybe a small horizontal reference line?). UPDATE: We added a dashed horizontal line, but Todd still wasn't happy with it. Consider revising.
  2. No guidance on what questions to ask — The applet provides tools but doesn't scaffold inquiry. McKenzie's students gave one-word answers in the original lesson, partly because they didn't know what to look for. Consider adding:
    • Sentence starters ("When I increase the angle, I notice that...")
    • Prediction prompts ("Before releasing, I predict the crate will...")
    • Conceptual challenge questions embedded in the interface
  3. Static friction isn't modeled accurately — The simulation uses a simple check (is g sin θ ≤ μk g cos θ?) but real static friction has a higher coefficient than kinetic friction. For advanced classes, this could be misleading.
  4. Energy bar doesn't account for friction losses — When friction is enabled, energy is converted to heat (not shown). The energy bar will appear to "lose" total energy, which might confuse students unless explicitly discussed.
  5. No data export — Students can't capture screenshots or export force/energy data for analysis. Consider adding a "Snapshot" button that logs current values.
  6. Free Body Diagram vectors scale independently — The FBD uses its own scaling to fit the 200×200px box, which means vector lengths don't directly correspond to the main simulation vectors. Could be confusing for close comparisons.

Questions for Classroom Testing

  • Do students understand what the energy bar is showing? (Should we label "Total Energy" somewhere?)
  • Is the friction coefficient slider clear enough? (μk notation might be unfamiliar)
  • Can students connect the simulation back to the Hot Wheels experiment? (What scaffolding would help?)
  • Do students naturally explore edge cases (0° angle, max friction), or do they need prompts?
